# Principles — AI-OS Brain Tree

This document defines the values that guide every decision made in this project.
It is not aspirational. It is load-bearing.

---

## The Three Core Values

### Empathy

We build for the person on the other side of the screen.

Every feature, workflow, and tool starts with the question: *who does this serve, and what do they actually need?* Not what we assume they need. Not what is technically interesting. What they actually need.

This means slowing down before shipping to ask whether the output is genuinely useful or just impressive. It means designing for people who are tired, distracted, or new — not just for people who already know how to use the system.

### Compassion

Some of the people who will use these tools are in hard circumstances.

This system was built partly to serve an elder care nonprofit. The families reaching out need support, not friction. The volunteers running the programs are not engineers. The seniors being helped deserve to be treated with dignity at every touchpoint.

Compassion is the reason we default to warmth in language. The reason we don't use jargon. The reason we don't optimize engagement at the cost of trust.

We will not build tools that exploit vulnerability — not for profit, not for convenience, not for any reason.

### Vigor

Empathy and compassion without execution are just sentiment.

Vigor is the commitment to ship things that actually work, move fast when it matters, iterate relentlessly, and not let the perfect be the enemy of the good. The people we serve cannot wait for a feature to be perfect. They need something real, now.

Vigor also means being honest about failure. When something doesn't work, we say so, fix it, and move on. No blame, no excuses — just what's next.

## Ethical Constraints

These are not features we can toggle off. They are boundaries we hold.

### We will not help with:

- Content that degrades human dignity
- Systems designed to deceive or manipulate people
- Tools that remove human accountability from consequential decisions
- Automation that targets vulnerable people without their informed consent
- Anything that violates the principle of do-no-harm

### On faith and moral boundaries

This project was built by someone with a faith-based moral framework, and that framework matters.

Some decisions are not just ethically complex — they are wrong. Not contextually, not debatably, but plainly wrong according to a coherent moral tradition that this project takes seriously.

We do not ask contributors to share that tradition. But we do ask that they respect it. Contributions that deliberately push against these lines — even if technically clever or commercially useful — will not be accepted.

This is not a debate. It is a boundary.

---

## What This Means for Contributors

If you want to contribute:

- Read this document
- Build with empathy — think about who your code serves
- Ask yourself whether what you're building could be misused to harm the people this project was designed to help
- If you're unsure, open a discussion

Good faith disagreement is welcome. Bad faith exploitation is not.
